Part 2: Nerve Conduction and Synapse

Synaptic Transmission

The process by which a nerve impulse (action potential) is passed from one neuron to the next (or to an effector cell like a muscle) across a synapse.

Presynaptic Neuron

The neuron that sends the signal, releasing neurotransmitters from its axon terminal.

Postsynaptic Neuron

The neuron that receives the signal, possessing receptor sites for the neurotransmitters on its dendrite or cell body.

Synaptic Vesicles

Contain Neurotransmitters (e.g., Acetylcholine, Dopamine, Glutamate). These are chemical messengers that diffuse across the synaptic cleft to transmit the signal.

What should be the reason for a nerve impulse to be conducted?
For a nerve impulse to be generated and conducted, the stimulus must be strong enough to reach the threshold potential. This is the minimum level of depolarization required to open voltage-gated sodium channels and trigger an all-or-none action potential.
Depolarization
The phase where the inside of the neuron becomes less negative (more positive) compared to the outside. This occurs due to the rapid influx of Sodium (Na⁺) ions into the axon.
Repolarization
The phase where the membrane potential returns to its resting negative value after depolarization. This occurs due to the efflux of Potassium (K⁺) ions out of the axon.
Saltatory Conduction
Saltatory conduction is the mode of nerve impulse conduction in myelinated axons. The impulse "jumps" from one Node of Ranvier (gaps in the myelin sheath) to the next. This is much faster and more energy-efficient than conduction in unmyelinated axons.

Part 3: The Human Brain

Structure of the Human Brain

Forebrain

  • Cerebrum: Responsible for higher functions like thought, memory, reasoning, and voluntary actions. Its surface is the cerebral cortex.
  • Thalamus: Relays sensory and motor signals to the cortex.
  • Hypothalamus: Regulates body temperature, hunger, thirst, and the pituitary gland.

Midbrain

  • Connects the forebrain and hindbrain
  • Involved in vision, hearing, and motor control

Hindbrain

  • Cerebellum: Coordinates voluntary movement, posture, and balance.
  • Pons: Relays signals and is involved in sleep and respiration.
  • Medulla Oblongata: Controls vital involuntary functions like breathing, heart rate, and blood pressure.

Lobes of the Cerebrum

Frontal Lobe

Associated with reasoning, planning, parts of speech, movement, emotions, and problem-solving.

Parietal Lobe

Processes sensory information like touch, temperature, and pain (somatosensory cortex).

Temporal Lobe

Involved in auditory perception (hearing) and memory.

Occipital Lobe

The primary visual processing center of the brain.

Part 4: Nervous Disorders & Other Systems

Nervous Disorders

Parkinson's Disease
A progressive neurodegenerative disorder caused by the loss of dopamine-producing neurons in the substantia nigra (part of the midbrain). Symptoms include tremors, muscle rigidity, slowness of movement (bradykinesia), and impaired balance.
Alzheimer's Disease
A progressive neurodegenerative disorder and the most common cause of dementia. It is characterized by the accumulation of amyloid plaques and neurofibrillary tangles in the brain. Symptoms include memory loss, confusion, disorientation, and changes in personality.

Part 5: Brain Areas and Endocrinology

Brain Areas

Association Area
Regions of the cerebral cortex that integrate and process information from the primary sensory and motor areas. They are involved in complex functions like learning, memory, and thought.
Broca's Area
Located in the frontal lobe (usually left hemisphere), it is responsible for speech production. Damage results in Broca's aphasia (difficulty speaking but can understand language).
Wernicke's Area
Located in the temporal lobe (usually left hemisphere), it is responsible for language comprehension. Damage results in Wernicke's aphasia (fluent but nonsensical speech and poor comprehension).

Hormones

Functions Hormone Glands
1) Helps in relaxing pelvic ligaments to facilitate easy birth. Relaxin Ovary
2) Growth of thyroid gland and secretion of thyroxine hormone. TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone) Pituitary (Anterior)
3) Controls calcium level in the blood. Calcitonin and Parathormone (PTH) Thyroid (Calcitonin), Parathyroid (PTH)
4) Controls tubular absorption of water in kidney. ADH (Antidiuretic Hormone/Vasopressin) Hypothalamus (produced), Pituitary (stored & released)
5) Secretion of growth hormone. GHRH (Growth Hormone-Releasing Hormone) Hypothalamus
6) Development of immune system and maturation of T-lymphocyte. Thymosin Thymus

Part 6: Short Answer Questions

Largest part of the brain

Cerebrum

Three structures that form the limbic system

Hypothalamus, Amygdala, and Hippocampus. (The thalamus is sometimes included as part of the limbic system).

Name the parts of the adenohypophysis

The adenohypophysis (anterior pituitary) includes the pars distalis, pars intermedia, and pars tuberalis.

The fluid in the eye between the lens and cornea

Aqueous Humor

The receptor that is sensitive to mechanical stimuli like touch and pain

Mechanoreceptor (for touch/pressure). (Note: Pain is detected by Nociceptors).
